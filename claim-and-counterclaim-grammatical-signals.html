The current COVID-19 crisis has obliged most education systems to adopt alternatives to face-to-face teaching and learning. Many education systems moved activities online, to allow instruction to continue despite school closures.

Considering the alternative of no schooling, online schooling has been an important tool to sustain skills development during school closures. That being said, there are still concerns that online learning may have been a sub-optimal substitute for face-to-face instruction, especially so in the absence of universal access to infrastructure (hardware and software) and lack of adequate preparation among teachers and students for the unique demands that online teaching learning pose.

28. Directions: Reflect and answer each of the following questions.1. What are transition signals or grammatical signals? 2. How do we use grammatical signals on? A. Claim and Counterclaim B. Cause and Effect C. Problem-Solution D. Emphasis​


Answer:

1. What are transition signals or grammatical signals?

Transition signals, also known as transitional words or phrases, are words or phrases that connect ideas and sentences within a paragraph or between paragraphs. They help to show the relationship between ideas and guide the reader through the text.

2. How do we use grammatical signals on?

A. Claim and Counterclaim

B. Cause and Effect

C. Problem-Solution

D. Emphasis

Grammatical signals can be used in various ways to help organize and structure writing. Here are some examples:

A. Claim and Counterclaim:

In this type of writing, words like "however," "although," "nevertheless," "on the other hand," and "despite" can be used to signal a shift in the argument or a counterargument.

B. Cause and Effect:

Words like "therefore," "thus," "consequently," "as a result," and "due to" can be used to show the cause-and-effect relationship between ideas.

C. Problem-Solution:

Words like "solution," "problem," "resolve," and "address" can be used to signal a problem and a proposed solution.

D. Emphasis:

Words like "indeed," "in fact," "clearly," and "obviously" can be used to emphasize a point or idea.
