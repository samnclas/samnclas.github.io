15. critique of slumdog millionaire​


"Slumdog Millionaire" is a movie that tells the story of a young boy named Jamal who grows up in the slums of Mumbai and later participates in the Indian version of "Who Wants to Be a Millionaire?". While the film has been praised for its engaging storyline and portrayal of Indian culture, a Marxist critique would reveal certain problematic themes and implications about the film's representation of class and power dynamics.

One of the most significant criticisms of "Slumdog Millionaire" is its portrayal of poverty and the slums. While the movie provides a window into the struggles and realities of poverty in India, it also perpetuates the idea that the slums are a place of hopelessness and despair. The film presents the slums as a place of constant violence, crime, and exploitation, which reinforces stereotypes about poverty and the poor. This portrayal is problematic, as it fails to acknowledge the structural causes of poverty, such as inequality, exploitation, and corruption, which are rooted in the economic and political systems.

Moreover, the film's central theme of Jamal's participation in a quiz show and subsequent rise from poverty to wealth reinforces the idea of the "American Dream" - the notion that anyone can achieve success through hard work and determination. This idea ignores the fact that social mobility and economic opportunities are often constrained by social and economic structures. The movie's message of individual achievement and success thus ignores the systemic inequalities that limit upward mobility for many in society.

Another critique of "Slumdog Millionaire" is its representation of women. The film portrays women as secondary characters, whose roles are often limited to providing emotional support to male characters. Moreover, the portrayal of women is often sexualized and objectified, which reinforces patriarchal norms and gender inequality.

In conclusion, "Slumdog Millionaire" may have been a commercial success, but it is not immune to critique from a Marxist perspective. The film perpetuates stereotypes about poverty and reinforces the idea of the "American Dream" while ignoring the systemic inequalities that limit social mobility. The representation of women in the film is also problematic, as they are often relegated to secondary roles and sexualized.
