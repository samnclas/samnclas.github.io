4. The contact between the armature and the source of power in a motor?A.BrushesB.Force fieldC.ferromagneticD.Electromagnet​


Brushes make contact between the armature and the power supply of a motor.

A commutator is defined as a spinning electrical switch in various types of electric motors and generators that periodically reverses the current trajectory between both the rotor and the external circuit. It includes a cylinder made up of multiple metal contact sections that is fixed on the spinning armature of the machine.

The armature is constructed from wire windings wrapped around over a ferromagnetic core. When an electric current runs through the wire, the magnetic field from the field magnet produces a force (Lorentz force) on it, spinning the rotor and generating mechanical output. When triggered with electricity, windings are coils of wire coiled around a layered, flexible, metal, ferromagnetic core to form magnetic poles.

Most electric motors generate force in the form of torque applied to the motor's blade by reacting between the motor's magnetic field and electric current in a wire coil. An electric generator is mechanically similar to an electric motor, but it operates with a reversed flow of power, producing mechanical energy into electrical energy.

Electric motors can be powered with direct current (DC) supplies like batteries or buck converters or alternating current (AC) supplies including an electricity system, inverters, or power grid.

The two mechanical components of an electric motor are the rotor, which rotates, and the stator, which does not. It also includes two electrical components: a set of magnets and an armature, among which is linked to the rotor while the other to the stator to form a magnetic circuit.

13. What wi happen to the rotation of the motor if the number of loops in the armature is decreased?


Answer:

Motors are the most common application of magnetic force on current-carrying wires. Motors have loops of wire in a magnetic field. When current is passed through the loops, the magnetic field exerts torque on the loops, which rotates a shaft. Electrical energy is converted to mechanical work in the process.

The torque found in the preceding example is the maximum. As the coil rotates, the torque decreases to zero at θ = 0. The torque then reverses its direction once the coil rotates past θ = 0. (See Figure 2(d).) This means that, unless we do something, the coil will oscillate back and forth about equilibrium at θ = 0. To get the coil to continue rotating in the same direction, we can reverse the current as it passes through θ = 0 with automatic switches called brushes.

Meters, such as those in analog fuel gauges on a car, are another common application of magnetic torque on a current-carrying loop. Figure 4 shows that a meter is very similar in construction to a motor. The meter in the figure has its magnets shaped to limit the effect of θ by making B perpendicular to the loop over a large angular range. Thus the torque is proportional to I and not θ. A linear spring exerts a counter-torque that balances the current-produced torque. This makes the needle deflection proportional to I. If an exact proportionality cannot be achieved, the gauge reading can be calibrated. To produce a galvanometer for use in analog voltmeters and ammeters that have a low resistance and respond to small currents, we use a large loop area A, high magnetic field B, and low-resistance coils.

What is armature efficiency?

The ratio of the output power to the input power is the simplest definition of efficiency. Let R represent the circuit's overall resistance (including the brush contact resistance, at series winding resistance, inter-pole winding resistance and compensating winding resistance). The ratio of output power to input power is the definition of a DC motor's efficiency.

The majority of electric motors are made to operate between 50% and 100% of their rated load. Typically, maximum efficiency is within 75% of the rated load. Therefore, the allowable load range for a 10-horsepower (hp) motor is between 5 and 10 hp; its peak efficiency is at 7.5 hp. Below roughly 50% load, a motor's efficiency tends to decline significantly. The range of good efficiency, however, differs for each motor and has a tendency to cover a wider range for larger motors.

When a motor is in the range where efficiency dramatically decreases with decreasing load, it is said to be underloaded. As load diminishes, the power factor tends to decline more quickly but less abruptly than efficiency.
